Projects And Publications

Projects

The Research Institute in Science of Cyber Security (RISCS) is a research institute which is managed by a team based at University College London. According to its mission statement “RISCS seeks to bring together academic researchers and security practitioners to work on real-world security problems and in so doing produces scientific understanding of the intersection between threats to information and threats to people and organisations”. [1]

It is now in its second ‘phase’, which will run over the next five years. The following is a list of projects at RISCS along with their aims:

-- Cyber-Security across the Life Span (cSaLSA) – To investigate the differences in the kinds of cyber security risks faced by people in different stages of life.

-- Addressing Cybersecurity and Cybercrime via a co-Evolutionary aPproach to reducing human-relaTed risks (ACCEPT) - To study the evolution of both cybercrime and cybersecurity in order to be able to influence the actions of people in both areas to effectively reduce certain cybercrime risks.

-- Why Johnny doesn’t write secure software: secure software development by the masses – To understand which kinds of security mistakes are most commonly made by developers, why these mistakes happen and how the issues caused by these mistakes can be managed.

-- Detecting and Preventing Mass-Marketing Fraud - To develop new techniques for defending against online Mass-Marketing Fraud, a type of scheme in which online mass-communication tools such as email and social media are used to commit scams involving a large number of victims.

-- Motivating Jenny to write secure software - To study the role which industry culture plays in motivating developers to employ certain software development practices which are beneficial to the security of their software, and how a culture in which security is taken seriously can be developed and sustained.

-- Evaluating Cyber Security Evidence for Policy Advice: the other human dimension – To understand the challenges faced by policy makers in government when assessing evidence on issues related to cyber security. This project will involve an analysis of these challenges and a set of recommendations for how these challenges can be overcome.

-- EconoMical, PsycHologicAl and Societal Impact of RanSomware (EMPHASIS) – To understand the exact effects of ransomware (a type of malware) on both a societal level and on an individual level.

-- CASCAde (Confidentiality-Preserving Security Assurance) – To create a new type of security assurance (the process of ensuring that a certain system is secure) which is itself secure, and does not reveal sensitive information about the system and how it works.

Noteworthy recent papers from UCL researchers

Melis, L., Danezis, G., De Cristofaro, E. (2016). Efficient Private Statistics with Succinct Sketches.
This paper earned the authors, Luca Melis, Prof George Danezis and Dr Emiliano De Cristofaro, members of the UCL Department of Computer Science, the “Data Protection by Design Award” from The Catalan Data Protection Authority. The award gives recognition to technological solutions that make a relevant contribution to the protection of privacy in the design of applications.

Olejnik, L., Acar, G., Castelluccia, C., Diaz, C. (2015). The leaking battery A privacy analysis of the HTML5 Battery Status API.
Due in part to this paper by Dr Olejnik, a research associate in the UCL Department of Computer Science, the web browser Firefox and the engine WebKit (the engine behind Safari and Google Chrome) have removed support for the Battery API in their browsers.
