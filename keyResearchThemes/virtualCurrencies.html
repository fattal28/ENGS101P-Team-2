Virtual Currencies

What are Virtual Currencies?

A virtual currency is a type of unregulated, digital money, which is issued and usually controlled by its developers, and used and accepted among the members of a specific virtual community.[1] It is commonly associated with blockchain, which is a public digital record of all transactions made in virtual currencies. In recent years, virtual currencies have risen in popularity dramatically, especially with the growth of BitCoin.

Virtual currencies have existed since 1996, with E-Gold, a digital currency backed by gold, growing to be majorly successful for the time before falling victim to cybercrime and hacker attacks. Since then, a large number of virtual currencies have come to existence: many of which faded out; and a few which survived the test of time, such as WebMoney (since 1998).[2]

Due to the anonymous nature of modern virtual currencies, they play a major role in the field of information security. Despite offering great utility to customers, such as being able to donate to causes anonymously, they are facing public scrutiny for their use in cybercrime and illicit activities, such as the purchase of drugs on the Deep Web, or their use in terrorism.[3]

The Controversy Surrounding Virtual Currencies

“Optimists claim that Bitcoin will fundamentally alter payments, economics, and even politics around the world. Pessimists claim Bitcoin is inherently broken and will suffer an inevitable and spectacular collapse.”

Narayanan, A., Bonneau, J., Felten, E., Miller, A. and Goldfeder, S. (n.d.). Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency Technologies. Princeton University Press, p.8.

Virtual currencies have faced strong opposition from governments, due to their untraceable and unregulatable nature. Recently, the Chinese government, among many others, has banned the currency as tender – and the currency has fought back. “The ethos behind blockchain has been tested," says Ken Sangha, COO of Open Money and the Open Project in San Francisco. "A central, organized and powerful authority -- China -- said 'no' and we all have been tested worldwide because of it. But the system flexed its muscles. It's doing what it was supposed to do.”[6]

This is mainly due to the inherent nature of virtual currencies in that it is decentralised: it is impossible to regulate by central banks or governments. This results in wild volatility shown by the abrupt changes in prices of a currency below.[7]



On the other hand, virtual currencies offer a method of payment for the group of people who do not trust the government or large corporate entities, for any reason. They provide complete anonymity, which, to some, can feel safer.

Research and the Future

As the field or virtual currency expands, research into becomes increasingly important. UCL, as one of the world’s leading research centres[8], has set up the UCL Centre for Blockchain Technologies (CBT), specialised in the development of blockchain technologies.

“The UCL Centre for Blockchain Technologies (CBT) provides a cross-sectoral platform, which drives the adoption and integration of Blockchain and Distributed Ledger Technologies into our socio-economic system.”

http://blockchain.cs.ucl.ac.uk

With over 100 papers already published, the department is currently working on projects such as RSCoin, a virtual currency controlled and maintained by the central bank; and working on the EU-wide DECODE project, an ambitious, EU-backed project which explores the idea that data should be shared differently, and individuals should have instant access to all their personal data.
